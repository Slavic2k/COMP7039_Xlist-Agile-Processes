Nickel Facts

  • Nickel was discovered in 1751 by Swedish chemist Axel Fredrik Cronstedt.
  • The name "nickel" comes from the German word "kupfernickel" meaning "devil's copper".
  • Nickel is the 5th most common element on Earth, making up about 3% of the Earth's core.
  • It is highly resistant to corrosion and oxidation, even at high temperatures.
  • About 65% of all nickel produced is used to make stainless steel.
  • Nickel is magnetic at room temperature, one of only four elements that are.
  • The largest producers of nickel are Indonesia, Philippines, and Russia.
  • Nickel is essential for electric vehicle batteries, particularly in nickel-manganese-cobalt (NMC) batteries.
  • The U.S. five-cent coin (nickel) is actually 75% copper and only 25% nickel.
  • Nickel has a high melting point of 1,455°C (2,651°F).
  • Some people are allergic to nickel, which can cause skin irritation from jewelry or coins.
  • Nickel can be recycled indefinitely without losing its quality or properties.
